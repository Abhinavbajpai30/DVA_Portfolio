Section 6 of 8 — Problem Statements

Defining the Real Problems

Every problem statement below is pinned to a specific number from the data. These are not interpretations or assumptions — they are what the data directly shows. Each one leads directly into the solution in Section 7.

01
Workload Problem
Employees assigned overtime leave at 3x the rate of those who are not — yet the company continues assigning it without calculating the attrition cost it creates.
Evidence: 30.5% overtime attrition vs 10.4% non-overtime (chi-square, p < 0.001). 416 employees are on overtime. 127 leave per year as a direct result. Annual cost from OT-driven attrition alone: $10.9M.
02
Role Design Problem
The Sales Representative role is structurally broken — 2 in 5 leave every year — while Sales Executives in the same department retain at nearly twice the rate.
Evidence: Sales Rep: 39.8% vs Sales Executive: 17.5%. A 22.3 percentage point gap between two roles in the same department. The difference is not culture or management — it is the job design, quota targets, and career ceiling.
03
Compensation Problem
Employees who left earned $2,046 per month less than those who stayed — a gap so large and so statistically certain it leaves no room for doubt that pay is a primary reason people leave.
Evidence: Leavers avg $4,787/month vs retained avg $6,833/month. t-test returned p < 0.000001. Employees earning under $2K/month have a 54.5% attrition rate — more likely to leave than to stay.
04
Equity Problem
The majority of employees have no stock options and leave at 24.4% — yet one level of equity cuts that rate to 9.4%. The company has not expanded equity access despite this being the clearest retention lever in the data.
Evidence: Level 0: 24.4% vs Level 1: 9.4%. A 61% reduction in attrition from a single equity step. No other variable in the dataset produces a reduction this large from one unit of change.
05
Early Career Problem
Employees aged 18 to 25 and those in their first two years at the company are leaving far above the average rate — indicating that onboarding, early pay, and visible career progression are all insufficient.
Evidence: 18 to 25 age group: 34.8% attrition. Leavers average 5.1 years tenure versus 7.4 for those who stayed. The first two years are the highest-risk window — and appear largely unmanaged by current HR programmes.
Section 7 of 8 — How to Fix Each Problem

Specific Solutions for Each Problem

Each solution maps directly to a problem statement above. These are not general suggestions — they are concrete actions with expected outcomes calculated from the data. Start with overtime and equity: together they address 73% of the total attrition cost.

01
Solving the Workload Problem
Conduct an Overtime Audit and Build a Burnout Prevention Policy
The aim is not to eliminate overtime — it is to stop assigning it to the people most likely to leave because of it. The first step is knowing which overtime is genuinely essential versus which is avoidable.
Action Steps
01.
Audit every current OT assignment. Split them into essential (no alternative exists) and discretionary (workload-driven and replaceable). Target the discretionary category first — removing it costs the business nothing in output while reducing attrition immediately.
02.
Set a monthly overtime cap per role. Research consistently points to 10 hours per month as the threshold beyond which burnout and attrition risk spike. Anything above this cap should require written manager approval with a documented reason.
03.
Introduce flex time or comp-off for unavoidable overtime. Employees who must work extra hours should receive equivalent time back. This directly addresses the work-life balance score gap — leavers score consistently lower on this dimension than people who stay.
04.
Prioritise single employees in overtime reduction. Single employees on overtime leave at 49.6%. When workload redistribution is needed, single employees in high-overtime roles should be the first to have their hours reduced — the data makes this targeting clear.
05.
Track overtime hours against quarterly attrition by team. Build a simple dashboard that makes the OT-to-attrition relationship visible to managers. When management can see the cost of overtime in attrition numbers, they make different decisions about how it is assigned.
Expected impact: 84 employees retained per year — $7.24M saved annually.
Timeline: 60 to 90 days for audit and policy rollout.
Difficulty: Low to medium — requires HR policy change and manager buy-in, not budget.
02
Solving the Role Design Problem
Redesign the Sales Representative Role from Quota Structure to Career Path
The fact that Sales Executives in the same department retain twice as well proves this is not a cultural or management problem. The role itself — its demands, its targets, its ceiling — is what is driving people out.
Action Steps
01.
Run stay interviews with Sales Reps who are still there, and exit interviews with those who left. Ask specifically what makes the Executive role feel more secure. The answer will point directly at whether the issue is quota pressure, base pay, growth visibility, or manager ratio.
02.
Audit and recalibrate quota targets. If Reps are consistently missing targets while Executives meet theirs, the quota baseline is likely wrong for the Rep level. Unreachable quotas are one of the most well-documented attrition drivers in sales-heavy organisations.
03.
Define and publish a clear, time-bound promotion path from Rep to Executive. Spell out the exact criteria — revenue number, tenure threshold, skill requirements — and make it visible to every Rep from day one. Ambiguous career ladders are a primary reason entry-level sales roles lose junior talent before it can grow.
04.
Reduce the manager-to-rep ratio. Sales Reps need coaching, not just targets. A ratio above 1:8 leaves new Reps undercoached and undersupported. Pair high-attrition Rep clusters with closer senior oversight for the first 12 months.
Expected impact: Reducing Sales Rep attrition from 39.8% to ~20% saves ~17 employees per year — $1.5M annually.
Timeline: 3 to 6 months for redesign and rollout.
Difficulty: Medium — needs alignment with Sales leadership, not just HR.
03
Solving the Compensation Problem
Market Benchmark Every Role and Establish a Minimum Pay Floor Above $2,000 per Month
The $2,046 income gap is not explained by role differences alone — it is a competitiveness problem. The company's pay in the bottom two income quartiles is below what the market is offering, and people are leaving to collect the difference.
Action Steps
01.
Benchmark every role against current market rates. Use Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, or a consultant like Mercer. Focus particularly on Level 1 and Level 2 roles — that is where the gap is widest and the attrition is highest. Do this annually, not once.
02.
Set a company-wide minimum pay above $2,000 per month. The data shows 54.5% attrition below this threshold. Raising the floor does not require raising everyone's pay — it specifically targets the income band where attrition is a majority outcome and the cost of inaction is clearest.
03.
Introduce annual salary reviews tied to market data, not just performance scores. If the market moves and the company's pay does not follow, people leave to close the gap themselves. The bottom quartile of salaries should be reviewed against external benchmarks every 12 months as a minimum.
04.
Communicate total compensation clearly to high-risk groups. Employees who do not understand the full value of their package (base, bonus, benefits, equity) often underestimate how they compare to the market. Clear communication of total comp reduces flight risk even before raises occur.
Expected impact: Raising sub-$2K salaries to market rates reduces attrition in that cohort from 54.5% to below 30% — saving ~21 people per year, worth $1.8M.
Timeline: 90 days for benchmarking, 6 months for full rollout.
Difficulty: Medium to high — requires budget approval, but the cost of inaction is higher.
04
Solving the Equity Problem
Launch a Targeted Stock Option Expansion — Starting with the Highest-Risk Employees First
Moving Level 0 employees to Level 1 equity cuts attrition by 61%. The cost of an equity grant is substantially lower than the cost of replacing the employees who leave without one. This is the best return on investment in the entire dataset.
Action Steps
01.
Identify Level 0 employees in high-risk profiles and grant them Level 1 options in the first wave. Start with Single + Overtime employees at Level 0. This is the most dangerous overlap in the data — they face the highest attrition risk while also having the lowest equity stake. Target this group first, before expanding more broadly.
02.
Set a 2-year vesting cliff. The grant only pays out if the employee stays for 24 months. This creates a direct financial reason to stay through the highest-risk early-tenure window — which the data shows runs from 0 to 5 years of company tenure.
03.
Communicate the value of the grant clearly at the time it is issued. Employees who do not understand what their equity is worth treat it as worthless. A one-page plain-English summary — what the option is, what it could realistically be worth, when it vests — dramatically increases its retention effect at zero additional cost.
04.
Expand to all remaining Level 0 employees within 12 months. The first wave addresses the most urgent risk. The second wave makes Level 1 equity a standard part of the compensation architecture for everyone. This becomes the new baseline, not a special retention measure.
Expected impact: 90 employees retained per year — $7.76M saved annually — the single highest-ROI intervention in the dataset.
Timeline: 30 to 60 days for approval and grant structure, immediate rollout after.
Difficulty: Low to medium — financial cost of equity grant is far below the replacement cost avoided.
05
Solving the Early Career Problem
Build a Structured 0 to 2 Year Retention Programme for Junior Employees
Leavers average just 5.1 years of tenure. Employees aged 18 to 25 leave at 34.8%. The first two years are the period of highest vulnerability — and based on the satisfaction data, the current onboarding experience is not doing nearly enough to build commitment and clarity in that window.
Action Steps
01.
Build a 90-day structured onboarding programme. Most current onboarding ends at 30 days. Extend it to 90 with a defined learning plan, weekly check-ins, and cross-functional introductions. Employees who feel genuinely integrated in the first three months are significantly less likely to leave in year one — this is well-established in retention research.
02.
Create a buddy system that pairs new hires with 3 to 5 year employees — not managers. Someone who has been through the same early-career uncertainty but is not in a position of authority. This addresses job involvement and relationship satisfaction scores, which are both lower in leavers, without adding management burden.
03.
Run mandatory stay conversations at 6 and 12 months — not performance reviews. A stay conversation asks what would make the person want to remain, what is frustrating them, and where they see their career in 2 years. It is proactive. It catches exit signals 60 to 90 days before a resignation letter arrives.
04.
Publish a written career path for every Level 1 role. Entry-level employees often leave not because the opportunity does not exist — but because they cannot see it. A simple document showing Level 1 to Level 2 criteria, a realistic timeline, and the skills needed removes ambiguity and gives people a reason to invest in the next 18 months rather than look elsewhere.
05.
Set a formal target: reduce 18 to 25 attrition from 34.8% to below 20% within 24 months. Track it quarterly. Assign it to a named HR business partner. Without a specific number, a named owner, and a tracked deadline, this kind of programme does not get prioritised alongside day-to-day HR work.
Expected impact: Reducing 18 to 25 attrition from 34.8% to 20% retains ~25 additional junior employees per year — $2.2M in replacement costs avoided annually.
Timeline: 60 days to design, 90 days to full rollout.
Difficulty: Low — primarily a process and culture change, not a budget issue.
